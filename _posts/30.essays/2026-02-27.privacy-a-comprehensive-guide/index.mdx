## Preface

I started this guide as a set of common sense advices "use ad-blocker", "don't use VPN" (I explain why later in details).
But starting to read and experimenting more, I myself got more and more curious, falling deeper into a privacy rabbit hole.
I ended up disabling or deleting most social media accounts, switching to de-googled Android phone as my daily driver, looking for alternative payment methods online (no, not crypto).
Buckle up, I will share what I have found. And yes — the truth will shock you.

## Your data is used against you

Corporations hoard your data only to recklessly leak it, or sell it. Criminals use it to steal your identity, hack your accounts, and scam you or your parents.
Look at what is happening across the world. Dictators are using data to track and arrest dissidents.
Countries at war are using data to identify and target both, soldiers and civilians.

### Behavior Manipulation

This case is obvious but the tech behind it is very sophisticated.
Say you are searching for vacation tickets.
Within 3 minutes _YOUR PARTNER_ will 'accidentally' see ads for hotels at that destination — “Last spot! Book now or wave your vacation goodbye.”

You pay 20% extra for the hotel by using 'free' web search — you are the product.

### Political Manipulation

Imagine a foreign actor pushing for a certain political party to win an election in your country.
You don’t even need to imagine — it’s happening.

They have a budget, and they want to target people who are most likely to be influenced. That's where your data comes in.
To sway 100 people, they could spend $1000 on TV ads or $1 on Facebook. Even if you think you are apolitical, open-minded, or resistant to propaganda, your personal data will still be used — not to target you, but to exclude you.
This makes manipulation far more effective.

You end up with a corrupted government — everybody loses.

### Spreading Hatred & Division

Want to divide people? First, you need to identify the clusters — who thinks what, where tensions exist, and how to amplify them.
Your data contributes big time.

### Discrimination

Same data, different problem.
It could be something as “harmless” as charging you a higher price for the same product based on your phone model.
Or something more serious, like denying you a job position or mortgage based on hidden factors derived from your digital footprint.

#### \* \* \*

Data collection en-masse sways elections and referendums, ignites riots and wars, and enables mass deportations.
It erodes democratic societies because it gives too much power. It is only a matter of time before this power is abused.

## Level 1: an easy win-win

These simple steps will not only protect your privacy but will also make your online experience more enjoyable.

### Stop giving your data

Don't use Google Chrome, or Microsoft Edge — they are spyware. They collect your data and actively resist ad blockers.
Instead, use [Firefox](https://www.firefox.com/) or Safari.

There are many privacy-focused browsers, Vivaldi, LibreWolf, IronFox, Brave, TorBrowser, etc. — they all have a tradeoff I will explain later.
Bottomline, they don't guarantee more privacy out the box.

Ad blockers are your best online friends. They make your browsing faster, protect you from malware, and fight against invasive tracking.
Use uBlock Origin, and Privacy Badger for Firefox.

If you are tech-savvy, consider setting up a Pi-hole.

Don't use Google Search, or Bing — try [Ecosia](https://www.ecosia.org/), or [Qwant](https://www.qwant.com/). Honestly?
Google was the king until they started to hide search results on the second page to show more ads.
Try competitors, and you will be pleasantly surprised.

### Stop oversharing

Remove and stop giving your phone number, address, real name, and other personal data from social media profiles and websites.
Your digital footprint is a network, some of your actions online identify you indirectly, but some data points (your phone number, email, real name, date of birth) are scream that it's you.

Delete accounts you don't use. Demand erasure of your personal data under GDPR, the Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF).
You don't need a lawyer for that, the biggest websites have a special form or link, others will comply if you send them an email to a dedicated GDPR address (the templates are plentiful online).

Never log into websites with your social media accounts.

Never use your phone number for 2FA; it compromises both your security and your privacy.

We're living in the age of raging LLMs (so-called AI), so don't share personal data with friendly people in your DMs.
Not even if they are hot.

Don't share your CV on LinkedIn or any other job platform without a screening call.
If you are a Software Engineer, you are a high-value target for both data collection and social engineering attacks.

### Delete apps from your smartphone

Most apps abuse your data. Even if you don't give them permission, even if you don't use them.

Facebook was caught eavesdropping using a microphone, even when the app was closed.

An app can periodically wake up in the background, WiFi or cellular connection reveals your approximate location.
It can scan your phone for other apps: Bank of America, Tinder, Guitar Tuner — this list already discloses too much about you.

An app can scan your photos for GPS metadata or faces. Of course, it will beg you to give permission to contacts, calendar, call logs...

Seriosly, you don't need 90% of the apps on your phone. Use the web version of Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, LinkedIn, etc.
Use Progressive Web Apps (PWAs) instead of native apps whenever possible. (Save a browser tab as an app icon on your home screen).

Most of these apps are just webpages running in their own browsers, eating your phone's memory battery, and stealing your data.

Use only essential apps: banking, messaging with push notifications, navigation, offline music and books.

## Level 2: where privacy starts

I will explain how tracking happens, and what mitigation strategies exist.

### Your inbox

Think of somebody reading all your emails — they know what flight you booked, for what car your insurance is, your medical appointments and prescriptions, your employment contract with salary, your bank details, and much more.

You have to ditch Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook, and other free big email providers if you want privacy.
Use privacy-focused email services like [ProtonMail](https://proton.me/mail), or [Tutanota](https://tuta.com/).

I would also recommend buying your own domain name, that way you always can change email providers without changing your email address, and you can create as many aliases as you want.

`john.doe43@gmail.com` —> `me@john-doe.email`, or `hello@john-doe.me`

### Your digital traces

Every advertiser sells and buys your data through data brokers.
Many sites collect & share this information: a user with `<advertising-id>` bought a shampoo `<brand>`.
Or even a user with `<advertising-id>` was reading a manual for `<brand>` washing machine.

How do they correlate the `<advertising-id>` with YOU?

### Primary data points: email, phone number...

Every time your log in with your email address or phone number, the website can identify you with 100% confidence.

They know plus sign trick: `johndoe43+amazon@gmail.com` and `johndoe43+linkedin@gmail.com` are the same address.
They understand own domains: `amazon@john-doe.email` and `linkedin@john-doe.email` are the same address.

Services like [SimpleLogin](https://simplelogin.io/), [addy.io](https://addy.io/), [Firefox Relay](https://relay.firefox.com/) or Apple's “Hide My Email” create unique email aliases that forward to your real inbox.
They make your identification harder, as long as you don't reveal your other unique data points.

#### Device/browser fingerprinting

Data brokers use your screen resolution, timezone, installed fonts, browser plugins, and other to create a unique fingerprint of your device and browser.
Some privacy-focused browsers actively fight against fingerprinting by randomizing or hiding the data but it's a double-edged sword.
Almost all privacy-focused browsers has unique fingerprints which makes you to stand out the crowd by using them.
They prevent advertisers from identifying you confidently, but bit by bit, advertisers collect more data points...

#### Cookies, super-cookies, trackers

Good news, these are very easy to beat. Bad news, nobody cares.

Firefox has a native extension called Multi-Account Containers that are individual sandboxes (read almost completely different browsers).
The use case is to separate your online activities: banking, shopping, work, study.

But nothing prevents you from using it to separate data hoarders: Meta (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp), Alphabet (Google, YouTube), Microsoft (LinkedIn, GitHub), Amazon (Amazon.com, Twitch).
You setup each container so that every time you open Facebook, it opens in the Facebook container, every time you click a link in your Facebook feed, it opens OUTSIDE the Facebook container.
This way, Facebook trackers only see your activity within Facebook.

#### Ditch media giants

or at least only use if necessary, to interact with your family, you can always catch up with your friends on privacy-respecting platforms.

Replace WatsApp with [Signal](https://signal.org/), Twitter with [Mastodon](https://joinmastodon.org/), Instagram with [Pixelfed](https://pixelfed.org/), Reddit with [Lemmy](https://vger.app/).

### CAUTION:

#### DO NOT use a VPN for privacy.

First of all, by using a VPN, you are trusting the VPN provider with ALL your traffic.
Not only your web browsing but also all your phone apps, your smart watch, and all home devices connected to the internet.

By using a VPN, you stand out from the crowd, making it easier for trackers to identify you.

Moreover, all modern browsers now support DNS over HTTPS (DoH), making your traffic encrypted and private. Use it instead of a VPN.

#### Pay close attention to DNS.

Same reason as above, DNS tells everything about your online activities: what websites you visit, what apps you use, what devices you have.

When setting up a WiFi router, never use Google's DNS servers (8.8.8.8, 8.8.4.4).
Better options are Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1, 1.0.0.1, or Quad9's 9.9.9.9.

Beware of Ad-blocking DNS services like AdGuard DNS, or NextDNS. It's not that they are bad, but same as VPNs — you give them all your traffic, so you must trust them 200% or more.

Be curious about privacy, search the Internet before you share. Check website ratings before signing up.

## Real privacy is extremely hard if not impossible!

Your forget to close a tab or close an app, connect to a VPN, and your identity is revealed.
You interact with reCAPTCHA, and the neural network tracking your mouse movements and keystrokes identifies you bit by bit.
This is not a panacea.

Google's reCAPTCHA (select all motorcycles images) doesn't actually care about the motorcycles you select.
The neural network tracks your mouse movements, keystrokes. It can detect that you are not a robot, yes. But with enough interaction, it can also identify YOU.

The best you can do is to poison your online data with contradicting information, and make it harder to build a clear profile of you.
