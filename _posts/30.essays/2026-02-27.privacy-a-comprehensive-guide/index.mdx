## Preface

I started this guide as a set of common-sense advice like "use an ad-blocker" or "don't use a VPN." (I explain why later)
The more knowledge I gained, the deeper I fell into the privacy rabbit hole, the сuriouser and curiouser it became.
And by сuriouser, I mean depressingly dystopian.

I started personal experiments to get firsthand experience: I disabled most social media, switched to a de-Googled Android phone, and started routing online payments through burner logic.

Privacy isn't about hiding secrets or avoiding targeted ads. It is an engineering problem. We have built a world where our defaults mean living in a glass house.
Your data will inevitably be breached, aggregated, and weaponized.
The question is no longer _if_ LexisNexis or Meta knows your precise location during the January 6th riots or any other day, but how much friction you are willing to apply to stop them from correlating it with your bank account, education, and your political views.

### Threat Modeling

This guide is for the average person, who is not a target of state-sponsored surveillance.

## Your data is used against you

Corporations hoard your data only to recklessly leak it, or sell it. Later, criminals use it to steal your identity, access your accounts, and scam you or your relatives.
Look at what is happening across the world. Democratic governments are being replaced by authoritarian regimes with direct support from tech giants, using the data they collected.
Authoritarian regimes are using the data to track and suppress dissidents, deport people en-masse, and export their surveillance machines to other countries.
State-sponsored actors are meddling in elections, spreading propaganda, and sowing chaos. Right-wing actors using the data to spread hatred solely for power and profit.

### Behavior Manipulation

This case is obvious but the tech behind it is very sophisticated.
Say you are searching for vacation tickets.
Within 3 minutes _YOUR PARTNER_ will 'accidentally' see ads for hotels at that destination — “Last spot! Book now or wave your vacation goodbye.”

You pay 20% extra for the hotel by using 'free' web search — you are the product.

### Political Manipulation

Imagine a foreign actor pushing for a certain political party to win an election in your country.
You don’t even need to imagine — it’s happening.

They have a budget, and they want to target people who are most likely to be influenced. That's where your data comes in.
To sway 100 people, they could spend $1000 on TV ads or $1 on Facebook. Even if you think you are apolitical, open-minded, or resistant to propaganda, your personal data will still be used — not to target you, but to exclude you.
This makes manipulation far more effective.

You end up with a corrupted government — everybody loses.

### Spreading Hatred & Division

Want to divide people? First, you need to identify the clusters — who thinks what, where tensions exist, and how to amplify them.
Your data contributes big time.

### Discrimination

Same data, different problem.
It could be something as “harmless” as charging you a higher price for the same product based on your phone model.
Or something more serious, like denying you a job position or mortgage based on hidden factors derived from your digital footprint.

#### \* \* \*

Data collection en-masse sways elections and referendums, ignites riots and wars, and enables mass deportations.
It erodes democratic societies because it gives too much power. It is only a matter of time before this power is abused.

## Part 1: an easy win-win

These simple steps will not only protect your privacy but will also make your online experience more enjoyable.

### Stop giving your data

Don't use Google Chrome, or Microsoft Edge — they are spyware. They collect your data and actively resist ad blockers.
Instead, use [Firefox](https://www.firefox.com/) or Safari.

There are many privacy-focused browsers, Vivaldi, LibreWolf, Mullvad Browser, TorBrowser, Brave, etc. — they all have a tradeoff I will explain later.
Bottomline, they don't guarantee more privacy out the box.

Ad blockers are your best online friends. They make your browsing faster, protect you from malware, and fight against invasive tracking.
Use uBlock Origin, and Privacy Badger for Firefox.

If you are tech-savvy, consider setting up a Pi-hole.

Don't use Google Search, or Bing — try [Ecosia](https://www.ecosia.org/), or [Qwant](https://www.qwant.com/). Honestly?
Google was the king until they started to hide search results on the second page to show more ads.
Try competitors, and you will be pleasantly surprised.

### Stop oversharing

Remove and stop giving your phone number, address, real name, and other personal data from social media profiles and websites.
Your digital footprint is a network, some of your actions online identify you indirectly, but some data points (your phone number, email, real name, date of birth) scream your identity.

Delete accounts you don't use. Demand erasure of your personal data under GDPR, the Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF).
You don't need a lawyer for that, the biggest websites have a special form or link, others will comply if you send them an email to a dedicated GDPR address (the templates are plentiful online).

Never log into websites with your social media accounts.

Never use your phone number for 2FA; it compromises both your security and your privacy.

We're living in the age of raging LLMs (so-called AI), so don't share personal data with friendly people in your DMs.
Not even if they are hot.

Don't share your CV on LinkedIn or any other job platform without a screening call.
If you are a Software Engineer, you are a high-value target for both data collection and social engineering attacks.

But deleting your data from a direct service is only half the battle.

### Fight Data Brokers

Companies like LexisNexis, Experian, or Acxiom scrape public records and aggregate them with leaked datasets.
They build shadow profiles of you and sell them as a service.
You can combat this manually through opt-out frameworks, or use automated services like [DeleteMe](https://joindeleteme.com/) or [Incogni](https://incogni.com/) to legally pursue these brokers into removing your data.

### Reconsider apps on your smartphone

Most apps abuse your data. Even if you don't give them permission, even if you don't use them.

An app can periodically wake up in the background, WiFi or cellular connection reveals your approximate location.
It can scan your phone for other apps: Bank of America, Tinder, Guitar Tuner — this list already discloses too much about you.

An app can scan your photos for GPS metadata or faces. Of course, it will beg you to give permission to contacts, calendar, call logs...

Seriously, you don't need 90% of the apps on your phone. Use the web version of Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, LinkedIn, etc.
Use Progressive Web Apps (PWAs) instead of native apps whenever possible. (Save a browser tab as an app icon on your home screen).

Most of these apps are just webpages running in their own browsers, eating your phone's memory battery, and stealing your data.

Use only essential apps: banking, messaging with push notifications, navigation, offline music and books.

In case of your desktop / laptop. Replacing native apps with web versions is a win-win. Push notifications would still work, UI for the most cases is identical.
And you don't need to run separate instances of Chromium browser for each app, meaning less memory and CPU usage.

## Part 2: where privacy starts

I will explain how tracking happens, and what mitigation strategies exist.

### Your inbox

Think of somebody reading all your emails — they know what flight you booked, for what car your insurance is, your medical appointments and prescriptions, your employment contract with salary, your bank details, and much more.

You have to ditch Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook, and other free big email providers if you want privacy.
Use privacy-focused email services like [ProtonMail](https://proton.me/mail), or [Tutanota](https://tuta.com/).

I would also recommend buying your own domain name, that way you always can change email providers without changing your email address, and you can create as many aliases as you want.

`john.doe43@gmail.com` —> `me@john-doe.email`, or `hello@john-doe.me`

### Your digital traces

Every advertiser sells and buys your data through data brokers.
Many sites collect & share this information: a user with `<advertising-id>` bought a shampoo `<brand>`.
Or even a user with `<advertising-id>` was reading a manual for `<brand>` washing machine.

How do they correlate the `<advertising-id>` with YOU?

#### email address

Every time your log in with your email address or phone number, the website can identify you with 100% confidence.

They know the plus sign trick: `johndoe43+amazon@gmail.com` and `johndoe43+linkedin@gmail.com` are the same address.
They understand custom domains: `amazon@john-doe.email` and `linkedin@john-doe.email` are the same address.

Services like [SimpleLogin](https://simplelogin.io/), [addy.io](https://addy.io/), [Firefox Relay](https://relay.firefox.com/) or Apple's “Hide My Email” create unique email aliases that forward to your real inbox.
They make your identification harder, as long as you don't reveal your other unique data points.

#### Use a Password Manager

This is not privacy advice per se, but without a password manager you will inevitably reuse passwords. And, without a password manager, maintaining unique email aliases becomes a nightmare.

A password manager (like [Proton Pass](https://proton.me/pass), [KeePassXC](https://keepassxc.org/), [Bitwarden](https://bitwarden.com/), or [1Password](https://1password.com/)) solves this.
A built-in password manager, like the one in Firefox or Safari, is better than nothing.

While we're at it, prefer passkeys over passwords whenever possible.
And store all your passport, ID, driver's license photos into the same password manager as well. Don't keep them in your photo gallery, or on your desktop.

#### phone number

Services like [SMSPool](https://www.smspool.net) provide single-use phone numbers for SMS verification.
You may also consider your local VoIP providers or a second phone number for one-time purchases.

#### other unique data points

Real name & date of birth. You may opt to use a pseudonym and a random date of birth on websites that don't require real information.

Home address & credit card / bank account is where we see how we get into a privacy dystopia. We trade our most sensitive data for convenience.

You can order packages to a locker or a pick-up point. You can use one-time prepaid cards from [Privacy.com](https://www.privacy.com/)(US only) or [Revolut](https://www.revolut.com/).
You could use gift cards. You can use cash. It's all not as convenient.

#### Device/browser fingerprinting

Data brokers use your screen resolution, timezone, installed fonts, browser plugins, and other to create a unique fingerprint of your device and browser.
Some privacy-focused browsers actively fight against fingerprinting by randomizing or hiding the data but it's a double-edged sword.
Almost all privacy-focused browsers has unique fingerprints which makes you to stand out the crowd by using them.
They prevent advertisers from identifying you confidently, but bit by bit, advertisers collect more data points. So it becomes a cat-and-mouse game.

#### Cookies, super-cookies, trackers

HTTP is fundamentally stateless. To remember that you are logged in, a website leaves a cookie — a small text file strictly bound to its specific domain. It was a harmless architectural fix.
Every time you visit `news.com`, the browser sends all cookies associated with `news.com` back to the server. This way, `news.com` can recognize you as a returning visitor, keep you logged in, and personalize your experience.

Then ad-tech weaponized it. If `news.com` embeds an invisible 1x1 image (a tracking pixel) or script from `ads.meta.com`, your browser is forced to make a side-request to Meta.
During that request, Meta drops its own third-party cookie. Because every major website embeds these same pixels, Meta essentially watches you traverse the entire internet, correlating your identity across distinct domains.

When privacy-conscious browsers finally started blocking third-party cookies, the industry simply shifted tactics. They introduced **CNAME cloaking**. Instead of loading the tracker from `meta.com`, the target website creates a DNS record pointing `analytics.news.com` directly to Meta's servers.
Your browser thinks it is communicating with the trusted first-party domain, completely bypassing your third-party cookie restrictions.

And when you clear your cookies to start fresh? Enter **super-cookies**. These aren't stored in your browser's cookie jar at all; they abuse fundamental web protocols to persist your identity:

- **ETags:** Designed for browser caching. A server assigns a unique ETag to a resource. Your browser then dutifully sends that exact tag "I have the version X cached, do you have a newer one?", unknowingly broadcasting a persistent unique ID.
- **HSTS (HTTP Strict Transport Security):** Designed to force secure (`https`) connections. A server tells your browser, "Only connect to me via HTTP**S** from now on."
  Your browser remembers this rule for that specific domain. Trackers exploit this by routing you through a series of hidden subdomains (e.g., `a.tracker.com`, `b.tracker.com`, `c.tracker.com`).
  They intentionally set the HSTS rule on some subdomains (representing "1") and omit it on others (representing "0"). Because your browser permanently caches these security rules, it inadvertently stores a unique binary tracking ID (`101`) that the tracker can read during your next visit. This persists even in Incognito mode.

The good news is that modern ad-blockers and privacy-focused browsers have caught up to most of these cookie-based exploits. The bad news is that the ad-tech industry doesn't care — they have already moved on to fingerprinting (above).
Merely clicking "Block Third-Party Cookies" in your settings is practically obsolete.

### Social media and the boring dystopia

You may have heard that deleting your Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and TikTok accounts is the best thing you can do for your privacy.
Just like with cookies, Meta looks at your deleted account and thinks: 'That's cute.'

It will of course limit the data you share voluntarily (your posts, photos, and messages [^watsapp]).

But your online activity and your behavioral profile will still be collected through people you interact with, and through the websites you visit that have Meta's tracking pixels.

I'm not advocating for deleting your accounts, I know it's hard.

Start by restricting your activity on these platforms. Take your friends to more privacy-friendly alternatives.

Replace WhatsApp with [Signal](https://signal.org/), Twitter (is anyone left?) with [Mastodon](https://joinmastodon.org/), Instagram with [Pixelfed](https://pixelfed.org/), Reddit with [Lemmy](https://vger.app/).

You can still use these platforms, and limit tracking by combining the knowledge about tracking.

Good news, Linux on Desktop is superior for the most part.
Seriously, I've been a Windows user since Windows 95. I've been a Linux user since 2005 or so.

Windows 7 SP1 was the GOAT, but Windows 11? Advertisements in the Start menu? Seriously, Microsoft?

But then, the switch doesn't come without a cost. Some software is only available on Windows, and some hardware doesn't have good Linux support.

Again, here's where convenience comes at the cost of privacy.

### Get rid of your smartphone

You can delete all the apps you want, but Apple and Google control the root layer. They collect telemetry, location heatmaps, and app usage stats before your screen even turns on.

My experience using a de-Googled LineageOS phone at the moment of writing (early 2026) is underwhelming.
I don't expect my mum would be able to use it. I don't expect anyone without a technical background to be able to.

I myself couldn't have survived without a backup iPhone. I made banking apps work (without NFC tap-to-pay ability), but I have lost access to government services.
Moreover, banking and government services are always a moving target, you are always one update away from losing access.

[GrapheneOS](https://grapheneos.org/) is a much smoother experience, but it is only available for Pixel phones which I do not own.
Still it requires a lot of technical knowledge, first to flash onto the device, and then to set up and maintain sandboxed environments.

I don't have any data to back this up, but for ease of use, I personally prefer Apple's (hardware company) iOS over Google's (advertisement company) Android.
Apple made a lot of privacy-focused moves in the last few years.

### Neural networks

Talking to chatbots reveals a lot of personal data in the process. It could be something obvious (like "what to do if a 2-year-old child has a fever?") or something very subtle — your choice of words, the way you structure sentences, the topics you talk about, and much more.

Google's reCAPTCHA (select all motorcycles images) doesn't actually care about the motorcycles you select.
The neural network tracks your mouse movements, keystrokes. It can detect that you are not a robot with 95% confidence, yes.
But with enough interaction, it can also identify YOU.

### Behavioral Biometrics

Platforms profile your typing cadence, mouse trajectories, device tilt (gyroscope), and scroll speed.
Tor Browser normalizes window sizes and reduces timer precision to spoof rendering metrics.

### Hardware & Local Surveillance

Retail stores use Bluetooth beacons and Wi-Fi probe requests to track your phone's MAC address (your device's unique identifier) as you walk down an aisle.
You need to either turn off Wi-Fi and Bluetooth when leaving the house, or ensure MAC randomization is strictly enforced by your OS.

## Part 4: Take our privacy back

Anonymous browsing is possible, just use [TorBrowser](https://www.torproject.org/). But at some point, you will need to log in somewhere, and your anonymity will be compromised.
The more I think about privacy, the more I am inclined to believe that maintaining two separate identities is the only realistic way.
For instance, using TorBrowser for anonymous browsing, and a Firefox or Safari with containers for banking, shopping, work, and communication.
For the second identity, you can poison your data with contradicting information: random date of birth, pseudonyms, random genders, and so on.

A contrary take is to use Google Chrome, but only with DNS-level blocking of trackers, without logging in, and default settings to maintain the most generic fingerprint possible.

### DO NOT use VPN services for privacy.

First of all, by using a VPN, you are trusting the VPN provider with ALL your traffic.
Not only your web browsing but also the activity of all your phone apps, your smart watch, and all home devices connected to the internet.

By using a VPN, you stand out from the crowd, making it easier for trackers to identify you.

A commercial VPN merely shifts who can see your data from your ISP to the VPN provider.
If your goal is true anonymity, routing through decentralized nodes via [TorBrowser](https://www.torproject.org/) is much more effective.

### Pay close attention to DNS.

Same reason as above, DNS tells everything about your online activities: what websites you visit, what apps you use, what devices you have.

When setting up a WiFi router, never use Google's DNS servers (8.8.8.8, 8.8.4.4).
Better options are Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1, 1.0.0.1, or Quad9's 9.9.9.9.

Beware of ad-blocking DNS services like AdGuard DNS or NextDNS. It's not that they are bad, but same as VPNs — you give them all your traffic, so you must trust them 200% or more.

DNS-level blocking of ads, trackers, and malware is very effective, though. You may consider setting up your own Pi-hole or AdGuard Home together with a WireGuard server on a VPS.

This option is the best of both worlds: you have full control over your DNS, your traffic is encrypted, and instead of known VPN servers, you have a generic VPS IP address.

### Real privacy is extremely hard if not impossible!

You forget to close a tab or an app, connect to a VPN, and your identity is revealed.
You accidentally log in with your real email address (autocompleted), and your account is burned; you cannot undo that.

My personal take is that privacy is much easier if you are young, don't drive a car, don't have a mortgage, and have plenty of free time to tinker with your devices.
The older you are, the more data points you collect, tinfoil hat or not, your healthcare provider will leak all your private information.

Another take is, the more people care about privacy, the harder it gets for companies to track and manipulate us.
Meta does not need to record your audio; their probabilistic models, tracking pixels, and social graph correlation are so aggressive that they can predict what you are talking about based on your location data, who you are standing next to, and what their browsing habits are.

Spread awareness about privacy and maintain basic online hygiene. Remember the oldest adage of the internet: if you are not paying for the product, you are the product.

Whether software giants are leveraging monopoly powers to squeeze money from you, bankrolling preferred candidates to secure favorable legislation, or inadvertently leaking your behavioral profile to oppressive regimes — the house always wins when you play by their default rules.

Be curious. Add friction to their models. Be able to say:
